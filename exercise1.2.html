Exercise: Prediction Quality in an Insurance Decision

Predictions: Theory and Practice

Author
Affiliation

Patrick Schmidt

University of Heidelberg

Exercise

Scenario

A company frequently rents cars for its employees’ business trips. On each trip, the car is either returned undamaged (\(y=0\)) or incurs damage (\(y=1\)). Before each rental, the company must decide whether to purchase damage insurance.

  • Insurance premium: €500 per trip
  • Cost of uninsured damage: €2000 (repair costs, administrative fees)
  • Insurance covers all damage costs (net cost of covered damage is just the premium)

A risk assessment system predicts the probability of damage before each trip. It outputs one of two values: \(\hat p \in \{0.2, 0.8\}\).

The prediction space setting

We model the prediction target \(y\) and the prediction \(\hat p\) as random variables with the following joint distribution:

\(y=1\) (damaged) \(y=0\) (undamaged) \(P(\hat p)\)
\(\hat p = 0.2\) (low risk) 0.05 0.45 0.50
\(\hat p = 0.8\) (high risk) 0.35 0.15 0.50
\(P(y)\) 0.40 0.60 1.00

Questions

Part 1: Decision problem

  1. Define formally: the prediction target \(y\), the action space, and the utility function \(u(a, y)\).

  2. Derive the optimal action \(a^\star(\hat p)\) as a function of the prediction. At which threshold does the decision flip?

  3. State the optimal action for each prediction value (\(\hat p = 0.2\) and \(\hat p = 0.8\)).

  4. Compute the expected utility \(E[u(a^\star(\hat p), y)]\) that the company achieves by following the forecasting system. Compare this to:

    • always purchasing insurance (ignoring the forecast)
    • having a perfect forecast (\(\hat p = y\))

Solution

a) Definitions

  • Prediction target: \(y \in \{0, 1\}\), where \(y=1\) means “damage occurs”
  • Action space: \(a \in \{\text{buy}, \text{don't buy}\}\)
  • Utility function:
\(y=1\) (damage) \(y=0\) (undamaged)
\(a = \text{buy}\) \(-500\) \(-500\)
\(a = \text{don't}\) \(-2000\) \(0\)

b) Optimal action

The expected utility of each action given belief \(\hat p\): \[E_{\hat p}[u(\text{buy}, y)] = -500\] \[E_{\hat p}[u(\text{don't}, y)] = \hat p \cdot (-2000) + (1-\hat p) \cdot 0 = -2000\hat p\]

The optimal action is: \[a^\star(\hat p) = \text{buy} \iff -500 > -2000\hat p \iff \hat p > \frac{500}{2000} = 0.25\]

The decision threshold is \(p^\star = 0.25\).

c) Actions for each prediction value

  • \(\hat p = 0.2 < 0.25\): \(a^\star = \text{don't buy}\)
  • \(\hat p = 0.8 > 0.25\): \(a^\star = \text{buy}\)

d) Expected utility

First, we need the true conditional probabilities from the joint distribution: \[P(y=1|\hat p=0.2) = \frac{P(y=1, \hat p=0.2)}{P(\hat p=0.2)} = \frac{0.05}{0.50} = 0.10\] \[P(y=1|\hat p=0.8) = \frac{P(y=1, \hat p=0.8)}{P(\hat p=0.8)} = \frac{0.35}{0.50} = 0.70\]

Expected utility for each prediction:

  • At \(\hat p=0.2\) (action: don’t buy): \[E[u(\text{don't}, y)|\hat p=0.2] = 0.10 \cdot (-2000) + 0.90 \cdot 0 = -200\]

  • At \(\hat p=0.8\) (action: buy): \[E[u(\text{buy}, y)|\hat p=0.8] = -500\]

Overall expected utility: \[E[u(a^\star(\hat p), y)] = P(\hat p=0.2)\cdot(-200) + P(\hat p=0.8)\cdot(-500) = 0.5\cdot(-200) + 0.5\cdot(-500) = \mathbf{-350}\]

Comparison:

Strategy Expected utility
Perfect forecast (\(\hat p = y\)) \(0.4\cdot(-500) + 0.6\cdot 0 = -200\)
Forecasting system \(-350\)
Always buy (ignore forecast) \(-500\)

The forecasting system yields €150 higher expected utility than always buying, but €150 lower than a perfect forecast.

Part 2: Sharpness

Compute the sharpness of the forecasting system: \[\text{Sharpness}(\hat p) = 1 - 4\, E[\hat p(1-\hat p)]\]

Interpret: is the system making bold or cautious predictions?

Solution

\[\text{Sharpness}(\hat p) = 1 - 4\,E[\hat p(1-\hat p)]\]

Since \(P(\hat p = 0.2) = P(\hat p = 0.8) = 0.5\):

\[E[\hat p(1-\hat p)] = 0.5 \cdot (0.2)(0.8) + 0.5 \cdot (0.8)(0.2) = 0.5 \cdot 0.16 + 0.5 \cdot 0.16 = 0.16\]

\[\boxed{\text{Sharpness} = 1 - 4 \cdot 0.16 = 0.36}\]

Interpretation: The sharpness is moderate. The predictions (0.2 and 0.8) are spread away from 0.5, indicating the system makes reasonably bold predictions. Maximum sharpness (= 1) would require predictions at 0 or 1; minimum sharpness (= 0) corresponds to always predicting 0.5.

Part 3: Calibration

  1. Compute the calibration function \(f(\hat p) = E[y|\hat p]\) for both prediction values.

  2. Is the prediction calibrated? If not, describe the pattern of miscalibration.

  3. Is the prediction unbiased? (Check whether \(E[\hat p] = E[y]\).)

  4. Does the miscalibration affect the decisions in Part 1? Why or why not?

Solution

a) Calibration function

\[f(\hat p) = E[y|\hat p] = P(y=1|\hat p)\]

Using \(P(A|B) = \frac{P(A, B)}{P(B)}\): \[f(0.2) = P(y=1|\hat p=0.2) = \frac{P(y=1, \hat p=0.2)}{P(\hat p=0.2)} = \frac{0.05}{0.50} = 0.10\] \[f(0.8) = P(y=1|\hat p=0.8) = \frac{P(y=1, \hat p=0.8)}{P(\hat p=0.8)} = \frac{0.35}{0.50} = 0.70\]

b) Is the prediction calibrated?

Calibration requires \(f(\hat p) = \hat p\) for all values of \(\hat p\).

  • \(f(0.2) = 0.10 \neq 0.20\) — not calibrated (overestimates damage risk by 0.10)
  • \(f(0.8) = 0.70 \neq 0.80\) — not calibrated (overestimates damage risk by 0.10)

Pattern: The system systematically overestimates damage probability. Both predictions are 0.10 higher than the true conditional rate.

c) Unbiasedness

\[E[\hat p] = 0.5 \cdot 0.2 + 0.5 \cdot 0.8 = 0.50\] \[E[y] = P(y=1) = 0.40\]

Since \(E[\hat p] = 0.50 \neq 0.40 = E[y]\), the prediction is biased (overestimates on average). This is consistent with our finding that calibration fails — recall that calibration implies unbiasedness, so a biased prediction cannot be calibrated.

d) Does miscalibration affect decisions?

No! The true conditional probabilities are:

  • \(P(y=1|\hat p=0.2) = 0.10 < 0.25\) — correct decision (don’t buy)
  • \(P(y=1|\hat p=0.8) = 0.70 > 0.25\) — correct decision (buy)

The miscalibration (predicting 0.2 instead of 0.1, and 0.8 instead of 0.7) does not flip any decision because the true probabilities remain on the same side of the threshold as the predictions. Miscalibration is costly only when it moves a prediction across a decision boundary.

Part 4: Accuracy

Compute the expected quadratic scoring rule (Brier score): \[S(\hat p) = E[s_{qsr}(\hat p, y)] = 1 - E[(\hat p - y)^2]\]

Solution

\[S(\hat p) = 1 - E[(\hat p - y)^2]\]

Using the joint distribution (each cell contributes its probability times the squared error):

\[E[(\hat p - y)^2] = \underbrace{0.05 \cdot (0.2-1)^2}_{=0.032} + \underbrace{0.45 \cdot (0.2-0)^2}_{=0.018} + \underbrace{0.35 \cdot (0.8-1)^2}_{=0.014} + \underbrace{0.15 \cdot (0.8-0)^2}_{=0.096}\]

\[= 0.032 + 0.018 + 0.014 + 0.096 = 0.16\]

\[\boxed{S(\hat p) = 1 - 0.16 = 0.84}\]

Part 5: Score decomposition

Decompose the accuracy into uncertainty (UNC), discrimination (DSC), and miscalibration (MSC):

\[S(\hat p) = \underbrace{S(p_R)}_{\text{UNC}} + \underbrace{S(p_C) - S(p_R)}_{\text{DSC}} - \underbrace{S(p_C) - S(\hat p)}_{\text{MSC}}\]

where \(p_C = E[y|\hat p]\) is the calibrated version of \(\hat p\) and \(p_R = E[y]\) is the constant (uninformative) prediction.

  1. Compute \(S(p_R)\).

  2. Compute \(S(p_C)\).

  3. State MSC, DSC, UNC and verify that the decomposition holds.

  4. Interpret: is the forecasting system adding value? What is its main strength and weakness?

Solution

a) Uncertainty: \(S(p_R)\)

The reference prediction is the marginal rate: \(p_R = E[y] = 0.40\).

\[S(p_R) = 1 - E[(0.4 - y)^2] = 1 - \left[0.4 \cdot (0.4-1)^2 + 0.6 \cdot (0.4-0)^2\right]\] \[= 1 - [0.4 \cdot 0.36 + 0.6 \cdot 0.16] = 1 - [0.144 + 0.096] = 1 - 0.24\]

\[\boxed{UNC = S(p_R) = 0.76}\]

b) Calibrated prediction score: \(S(p_C)\)

The calibrated prediction is \(p_C = E[y|\hat p]\), which takes values:

  • \(p_C = 0.10\) when \(\hat p = 0.2\) (with probability 0.50)
  • \(p_C = 0.70\) when \(\hat p = 0.8\) (with probability 0.50)

The joint distribution of \((p_C, y)\) is the same as \((\hat p, y)\) with relabeled predictions:

\[E[(p_C - y)^2] = 0.05\cdot(0.1-1)^2 + 0.45\cdot(0.1-0)^2 + 0.35\cdot(0.7-1)^2 + 0.15\cdot(0.7-0)^2\] \[= 0.05 \cdot 0.81 + 0.45 \cdot 0.01 + 0.35 \cdot 0.09 + 0.15 \cdot 0.49\] \[= 0.0405 + 0.0045 + 0.0315 + 0.0735 = 0.15\]

\[\boxed{S(p_C) = 1 - 0.15 = 0.85}\]

c) Decomposition

\[MSC = S(p_C) - S(\hat p) = 0.85 - 0.84 = 0.01\] \[DSC = S(p_C) - S(p_R) = 0.85 - 0.76 = 0.09\] \[UNC = S(p_R) = 0.76\]

Verification: \[S(\hat p) = UNC + DSC - MSC = 0.76 + 0.09 - 0.01 = 0.85 - 0.01 = 0.84 \checkmark\]

d) Interpretation

The forecasting system improves on the naive constant prediction: \[S(\hat p) - S(p_R) = 0.84 - 0.76 = 0.08 = DSC - MSC = 0.09 - 0.01\]

  • Discrimination (\(DSC = 0.09\)): The system’s main strength. It meaningfully separates high-risk trips (\(p_C=0.70\)) from low-risk trips (\(p_C=0.10\)), providing actionable information.

  • Miscalibration (\(MSC = 0.01\)): The system’s weakness is small. Although predictions overstate risk, the calibration error costs little in terms of score.

  • Key insight: This illustrates the principle of maximizing sharpness subject to calibration. Even with imperfect calibration, the forecaster adds value because its ability to discriminate between risky and safe trips (DSC) far exceeds the cost of miscalibration (MSC).
