Targets

Target variable

  • Target variable needs to be clearly defined

    • note: This is uncommon in daily communication and even businesses

    • ambigous statements are the norm

    • not cleary defined: “The US will set tarifs on trade.”

    • clearly defined: “Average trade tariffs (weighted by net worth) on December 31 2025 will be above 10%.”

  • Standard targets are

    • an event

    • a real-valued outcome

Target property for events

  • for an event prediction

    • we normally want to know the probability \(\hat p\) of the event

    • but could also ask for point prediction: yes/no

    • or for ranges of probabilities (e.g., “30-60%”)

  • we have argued that probabilities can be connected to decision making consistently

  • point predictions do not allow to weigh the utility differences

    • in some situations the risk of being wrong is higher in one direction

  • ranges of probabilities normally do not identify a best decision

    • but decision-making with imprecise probabilities is also a developped field

    • called decision making under ambiguity

    • e.g., maxmin preferences: \[a^\star([p_l,p_h])= argmax_a \min_{p \in [p_l,p_h]} E_p[u(a,y)]\]

Target property for reals

  • for real-valued targets

    • ideally we want a probability distribution

    • identified by cumulative distribution function (cdf)

    • or by probability density function (pdf)

    • requires distributional assumptions

    • or approximations (interval probabilities or quantiles)

  • As probability distributions for real-valued targets are complicated, we often use point predictions

  • How does a point prediction for a real-valued target look like?

    • “My life expectancy is 85 years.”

    • “Most likely I will die with 85.”

    • \(\hat y = x' \hat \beta_{OLS}= 85\) years

    • I will not live more than 85 years.

  • All those statements can be connected to a probability distribution

  • Point predictions for real-valued targets should be understood as properties of a probabilistic predictions

    • Mean: “My life expectancy is 85 years.”

    • Mode: “Most likely I will die with 85.”

    • Mean (will learn later why): \(\hat y = x' \hat \beta_{OLS}= 85\) years

    • Maximum/Minimum: I will not live more than 85 years.

  • More common are unspecified point predictions:

    • Unspecified point prediction: “I will die with 85.”

  • other typical properties of a distribution \(P\)

    • quantile \(q_\alpha\) at level \(\alpha\): \[q_\alpha(P)=\{c | P(y\le c) = \alpha\}\]

    • interval probability \(IP_c\) at point \(c\): \[IP_c(P)=P(y \le c)\]

    • \(\alpha\)-confidence intervals \(CI_\alpha\): \[CI_\alpha(P) = \{Interval | P(Interval) = \alpha \}\]

Questions

Things to consider

  • information content of answer

    • broadly: range of probabilities \(>\) probability \(>\) point pred

    • broadly: specific target property \(>\) unspecified target

  • ease of use

    • broadly: range of probabilities \(<\) probability \(<\) point pred.

    • often connected to accuracy of report

    • What is better: A bad probability prediction or a good point prediction?

  • bias and incentives that question generates

    • suggestive questions

    • framing can change answers

  • heterogeneity in target population

    • e.g., research in natural sciences more open to commit to probability predictions than public health experts (Morgan 2014)

Events

  • Point prediction

  • unspecified scales

    • less informative than probability and vague

Procedures

4 steps (Garthwaite et al. 2005)

  • setup

    • who is the expert?

    • what is the appropriate target?

    • train expert in elicitation

  • elicit

  • fit distribution

    • fit a probability distribution to measured properties

  • checks

    • Adequate? (If no, go back to elicit)

Who is the expert?

  • different experts may have different opinions

  • sometimes subject-matter experts are bad at statistics

    • a doctor in medicine

    • a business leader

  • experts have specific biases

    • an expert in public health probably thinks positively about public health interventions

    • an AI entrepreneur probably is optimistic about the technology

Checks

  • Are reports and probability distribution internally consistent?

    • partitions should add up to 100%

    • if \(E_1 \subset E_2\), then \(P(E_1) \le P(E_2)\)

  • Generate statements from probabilities and check with expert

    • “Is global temperature in 2030 more likely to rise above 4 degrees than to stay below 1.5 degrees compared to pre-industrial temperatures?”

    • “Do you expect a rise above 5 degrees to happen in 5 out of 100 cases?”

    • Does the temperature stay in the interval \([2,4]\) with likelihood of 50%?

Strategic thinking process

  • one can teach the subject-matter expert strategies that help to form better predictions

    • avoid biases

    • improve information content

  • we will discuss such strategies at the end of part II (“superforcasting”)

  • alternatively, one can take the prediction and do post-processing without the expert

    • standard for models (e.g., in weather forecasting)

    • recalibration (make uncalibrated prediction calibrated)

    • forecast combination (combine multiple predictions to a single, better, one)

Confidence, Second Order Uncertainty

  • generally a probability distribution reflects uncertainty

    • and we argued that it can/should support decisions under uncertainty

  • however, subjectively, experts often feel different degrees of confidence about the same probability

  • one famous example for second-order uncertainty is from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC)

    • used several frameworks over the years to express confidence

    • switched to probabilistic framing with five qualifiers: very low, low, medium, high, and very high

    • example: “In the Northern Hemisphere, 1983–2012 was likely the warmest 30-y period of the last 1,400 years (medium confidence).”

  • confidence / second-order uncertainty will also be key in model-based algorithmic forecasting (part III)

    • related to “model uncertainty” and “miss-specification”

  • for example a linear regression prediction:

    • the model gives a prediction \(\hat y\)

    • model also provides confidence intervals for \(y\) (aka “parameter uncertainty”)

    • but if the model is misspecified this uncertainty quantification can be wrong (“model uncertainty”)

Wisdom of the crowds

  • If there are multiple experts, one could elicit multiple predictions

    • How to choose or construct final prediction?

  • Wisdom of the crowds: Empirically, we often observe that the average of all predictions outperforms most individual predictions

  • Important caveats:

    • not always true (small crowd of superforecasters outperforms general crowd)

    • if performance of predictions is known, can be better to choose good performing predictions

Incentives and Markets

Scoring rules

  • so far we have taken expert opinion as given

    • expert may have the option to gather information and improve prediction

    • expert may not report truthfully

  • Solution: good incentives.

    • what payment structure is a good idea?

Payment with proper scoring rules

  • Consider the following contract structure

    • expert issues prediciton \(\hat p\)

    • target outcome \(y\) realizes

    • expert receives bonus \(s(\hat p, y)\)

  • What is a good choice for the payment function \(s\)?

    • proper scoring rules!

  • If

    • expert maximizes expected payout and

    • \(s\) is a proper scoring rule,

    then the expert issues their true belief \(p\).

Advantage of proper incentives

  • Truthful reporting is incentivized

  • Incentives reward the inclusion of additional information

    • Including more information increases the expected score for ideal predictions (Holzmann and Eulert 2014)

    • Formally: For two ideal predictions \(\hat p_1 = E[y|x_1]\) and \(\hat p_2 = E[y|x_1, x_2]\) it holds that \[S(\hat p_1) \le S(\hat p_2)\]

    • Thus, expert has incentives to include more information in prediction

The problems with scoring rules

  • the properties above hold only under narrow circumstances

  • Requirement: Outcomes need to be observable

  • Problem: risk aversion

    • if expert is risk averse, she is not maximizing expected payout, but expected utility from it

  • Problem: personal stakes

    • if expert has personal stakes (e.g., hedge fund manager) can hedge with prediction (i.e., bias towards unfavorable outcomes)

  • Problem: reputation

    • if predictive performance is public, expert has incentives to maximize reputation

    • can lead, for example, to overly extreme or confident predictions

    • media landscape favors extrem statements

    • public institutions (like professors) favor non-commited messaging
